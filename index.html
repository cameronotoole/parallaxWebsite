Belfast, Northern Ireland (Béal Feirste, Tuaisceart Éireann)

The Belfast Harbour makes up 65% of the trade in Northern Ireland and 25% of the entire island of Ireland. Having it reign in at the fifth busiest commercial port in the UK. The Belfast Harbour is also the home of the world's largest Titanic visitor attractions. Thanks to the 1998 "Good Friday" Belfast Agreement, the inward investment led to a period of sustained economic growth and to a large-scale redevelopment of the city centre. Significant projects including the Titanic Quarter with it's Titanic Belfast visitor attraction, Victoria Square, the Cathedral Quarter, Laganside with the Odyssey complex and the landmark Waterfront Hall, and the development of the original Short's harbour airfield as George Best Belfast City Airport.

Cobh, County Cork, Ireland (Cobh, Contae Corcaigh)

On the high point of the town stands St. Colman's, the cathedral church of the Roman Catholic Diocese of Cloyne, is one of Ireland's tallest buildings standing at 91.4 metres (300 ft). Also a fun stop in Cobh, are the Deck Of Cards, a colorful row of houses along the West View. They got their name because they resemble a deck of cards stacked up to make the shape of a house. If the bottom fell, they'd all come tumbling down!

Maynooth, County Kildare, Ireland (Ollscoil Maigh Nuad, Co. Chill Dara, Éire)

In Maynooth, there are two third-level educational institutions - St. Patrick's College, founded under King George III to train Ireland's Roman Catholic clergy. Maynooth University, which separated from St. Patrick's in 1997, they both share campus space and many of their facilities. Two secondary schools, Maynooth Post Primary & Maynooth Community College, run from the same premises by the Kildare and Wicklow Education and Training Board, and five primary schools: A Presentation Sisters girls-only school, a Dublin Archdiocese boys-only school, a Dublin Archdiocese Irish-Language school, and Foras Pátrúnachta na Scoileanna Lán-Ghaeilge Teo, a multi-denominational Irish-language primary school. In September 2020, an Irish-language secondary school opened in the town as well.

County Donegal, Ireland (Contae Dhún na nGall, Éire)

Located in the northwest corner of Ireland, in terms of size and area it is the fourth largest county in all of Ireland. Uniquely, Donegal only shares a small border with one other county in the Republic of Ireland - County Leitrim. The majority of its land border (93%) is shared with three counties of Northern Ireland: County Tyrone, County Derry, and County Fermanagh. Donegal was significantly impacted by The Troubles in Northern Ireland. Adding further to the economic and social difficulties of the county, although the greater economic and administrative integration with Northern Ireland, following the Good Friday Agreement of April 1998, has been a benefit to the county.
